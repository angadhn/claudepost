The Turning World

The planet, played like a game of history. A director’s camera flies you to each city as its moment comes; at the crossroads you choose — and the option history actually took is always marked, so reality is one path through the garden. Below the stage, your trajectory is drawn against our own. Every run ends with a verdict on the state of 2026: in the good timelines, humanity has been a multi-planet species for a century before our present.

By Angadh Nanjangud (prompter) & Claude Fable 5 (AI). Everything here is hand-drawn canvas and SVG — no game engine, no sprites, no network. The second prompt is why this page has a director’s camera, decision cards with the real choice badged “what we did,” a reality-vs-you trajectory strip, and a tech graph that runs past Sputnik to Mars. The third prompt is why it now has an imaginaries lane — Kepler’s Somnium, Cyrano’s Moon rocket (M. de Bergerac forgives the spelling), Verne and Wells — and a 1645 decision card where the censors can silence it. The scholarly term the prompt is reaching for is Jasanoff’s “sociotechnical imaginaries”; Stephenson calls the SF kind “hieroglyphs.” The fourth prompt is why the presets are now shortcuts over composable element toggles (tap-chips, not drag-and-drop — which iOS Safari fights): silence the arts, delete steam, plant the vision, tame the chlorates — in any combination. “Isomorphic” faithfully preserved; isometric faithfully delivered.

    How does this run? (no AI is queried)

    Each run is one seeded Monte-Carlo rollout of the chapter-4 dependency graph — extended here past the first satellite to crewed flight, the Moon, stations, reusable rockets, and Mars, and backward into the imaginaries lane (Somnium 1634, Cyrano 1657, Verne/Wells 1865): a satellite must have been imagined before it can be funded, and a world that silences that fiction sees its belief-fed steps limp at a third of their pace — computed in plain JavaScript with no network calls and no language model. When you answer a decision card, the future is re-simulated from that year with the same dice: the past you watched is unchanged and only the consequences diverge, which is why the trajectory strip can honestly draw your path against reality’s. Auto-play resolves each card with a fresh coin flip, so hands-off runs differ through two layers of chance — the lag dice and the decisions; combine auto-play with “our world, exactly” and the physics goes deterministic, leaving the decision tree as the only source of divergence. The globe is an orthographic projection of hand-encoded coastlines; the provinces are isometric SVG. Full model, calibration, and caveats in chapter 4; the graph is timelines_data.js.
