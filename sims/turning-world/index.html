The Turning World

The planet, played like a game of history. Compose a world from its elements — silence the arts, delete steam, plant a medieval sky-vision — and the outlook meter tells you what to expect before you press Run. A director’s camera flies you to each city as its moment comes; Kepler, Cyrano and Newton stroll their home provinces; every run ends with a verdict on the state of 2026. In the good timelines, humanity is multi-planet a century before our present.

What do these rows mean?

Mode is the master switch. “History, exactly” locks the elements to history’s settings (all off — you can see that in the row) and runs with no luck in the timings: every technology lands on its true date, satellite 1957, every time. “Build a world” unlocks the elements and runs each world with history’s natural luck — every technology’s time-from-possible-to-done wobbles about ±15% per run, which compounds down the chain, so even an unchanged world re-rolls into satellites anywhere from the 1890s to the 2020s.

World elements are independent counterfactual switches, and every combination is a valid world: silence the arts (no Somnium, no Cyrano, no Verne — belief-fed steps limp at a third of their pace), delete steam, plant the 1250 sky-vision (a funded motor programme, which also substitutes for missing literature), tame the chlorates. ↺ resets to our world.

The outlook meter is computed, not chosen: whenever you change the elements, the engine quietly runs a small ensemble of futures and places the needle by the median date of the first satellite — pessimism means the sky stays empty past our present; optimism means Mars before today. It is a forecast of the very simulation you are about to watch, so the end-of-run verdict should usually agree with it.

    How does this run? (no AI is queried)

    Each run is one seeded Monte-Carlo rollout of the chapter-4 dependency graph — extended here past the first satellite to crewed flight, the Moon, stations, reusable rockets, and Mars, and backward into the imaginaries lane (Somnium 1634, Cyrano 1657, Verne/Wells 1865): a satellite must have been imagined before it can be funded, and a world that silences that fiction sees its belief-fed steps limp at a third of their pace — computed in plain JavaScript with no network calls and no language model. The elements you pick reshape the graph before the run (blocked nodes, added programmes, stretched or shortened lags); “history, exactly” disables the dice so every date is the true one, while “build a world” re-rolls each lag ±15% per run. The outlook meter runs a small ensemble of the same simulation in advance and reports the median. The globe is an orthographic projection of hand-encoded coastlines; the provinces are isometric SVG. Full model, calibration, and caveats in chapter 4; the graph is timelines_data.js.

    What is cliodynamics? (the theory under all of this)

    Cliodynamics — Clio, the muse of history, plus dynamics — is Peter Turchin’s name for treating history as a dynamical system: instead of narrating events, you pick state variables (population, elite numbers, state capacity, war intensity), write down how they push on each other, and let the equations run. Its classic results are about cycles — why agrarian empires boom and bust on ~two-century “secular cycles,” why elite overproduction precedes instability — tested against databases of many societies (the Seshat databank) rather than one anecdote. The founding objection is also the honest one: history gives us a sample size of one, and historians rightly bristle at “laws.” The cliodynamic reply is that patterns recurring across dozens of polities are evidence of mechanism, and that a mechanism, once written down, can be perturbed — which is exactly what Stephen Jay Gould asked for with “replaying life’s tape,” and what Asimov’s psychohistory promised in fiction. Counterfactual simulation isn’t a party trick bolted onto cliodynamics; it is the whole point of writing history as math: an equation you cannot re-run with different inputs has told you nothing.

    And how is that generic tool used here, specifically?

    You’re right to suspect cliodynamics is generic — Turchin’s own models are differential equations over continuous social quantities, and none of them mention rockets. This page borrows the stance (history as a perturbable system) but swaps the substrate: our state isn’t population curves, it’s a technology dependency graph. The “weird links between history and math” are exactly three, all inspectable: (1) the graph — which technology needs which, taken from the historical record (no smokeless powder without industrial acids; steel’s converter accepts water power or steam, which is why deleting steam merely delays); (2) the lags — how long each step took from possible to done in our one history, used as the baseline cost of that step everywhere; (3) the multipliers — named social forces (a vision, a customer, a censor) that stretch or shrink lags. Everything else is dice: the Monte-Carlo jitter is contingency — and note that the dice quietly stand in for mortal humans. “Watt dies young and someone else builds the condenser a decade later” is a lag re-roll, which is why the cameo cards credit a seat rather than a person whenever an invention lands outside its inventor’s working lifetime: simultaneous discovery (Merton’s “multiples”) is the historical norm, so in this model people are seats. Propagation between eras is nothing mystical — a world where Cyrano is censored differs from ours only through those three channels, compounding down the graph. The full equations, calibration, and the model’s honest sins (sample size of one; the graph can’t imagine paths its authors didn’t draw) are in chapter 4.
